America's last great offshoring, to China in the 90s, reshaped the economy, hollowed out the Rust Belt, rewired global supply chains, and upended modern politics. It touched every facet of our lives, from cheaper goods at Walmart to the rise of a globalized economy, ushering in a new wave of modernity.

But the next offshoring won't look like this. No, the next wave won't be to a developing nation halfway around the world.

This wave of offshoring, the Final Offshoring, will come from an army of robots fueled by the greatest money printer of all time, the AI boom. 

It will push datacenters into orbit and onto the ocean floor, the "Atlantis Trade,” while robot fashion becomes a real industry because your robot is an extension of you. It will make the five-star hotel experience the default home experience, and give everyone access to patient, personalized care that was once reserved for the wealthy. It will unlock custom manufacturing at every quality level, a "Super-Etsy,” and finally make it economical to clean up potholes, rewild degraded ecosystems, and explore the deep sea.

The CPI will break because our inflation metrics assume labor-intensive services are expensive, and Georgism will become mainstream. A new class of robopsychologists who use mechanistic interpretability to peer into robot minds will emerge, vindicating Isaac Asimov sixty years later. But most importantly, it will pose the question we've never had the luxury to seriously ask: what do you actually want to do with your time, and who do you want to spend it with?

Robotics is new, fragmented, and raises challenging questions. But I believe those questions, posed to the right people, will usher a much better, crazier, and stranger world…and soon. This essay is an exploration of what that world, our likely future, means.

The Infinite Money Printer

“You Americans measure profitability by a ratio. There’s a problem with that. No banks accept deposits denominated in ratios. The way we measure profitability is in ‘tons of money’.” Morris Chang, founder of TSMC

Robotics has a market problem. At first, that might seem odd, as “$40T in potential addressable labor spend” is on every robotics startup pitch deck known to man. However, that number hides many painful realities. Most of that $40T is in fiercely competitive, low-growth, low-margin industries. One of those market characteristics would be enough to kill any startup. Combine all three and it’s no wonder investors who say they’re “excited about robotics” are more hesitant behind the scenes.

Of course, not all robotics companies compete in bad markets. Intuitive Surgical, the maker of the da Vinci Surgical System, is publicly traded and currently valued at a crisp $206 billion. However, Intuitive also operates in the highly regulated medical device industry and took 30 years to get to their current valuation.

Surgical robotics won’t save us.

But the AI datacenter buildout will.  

The Buildout

The US economy has transformed into a levered bet on AI CapEx, and that bet is likely to continue. ZIRP was a megatrend of macroeconomics; AI is a megatrend of technology. ZIRP died because rates rose and exposed how few companies were actually making money. AI is thriving because demand keeps growing faster than companies can keep up with, unlike ZIRP and also the “dark fiber” internet-era buildouts.

While yes, I am aware that a lot of "annual" "recurring" "revenue" claims are gamed, and that a large chunk of this "intermediate revenue" (such as from cloud credits, etc.) is going to hyperscalers that somewhere down the chain is funded by debt, it does not distract from the fact that real claims of ROI are coming out, enterprise adoption is only just beginning, and the hyperscalers are furiously spending to ensure they can get ahead of demand and their competitors, all of which are unlikely to stop.

The Counterobjections

Now, many point out that this could change. The objections roughly fall into three categories: model progress, infrastructure bottlenecks, and geopolitical risk. Let me address each in turn.

First, on model progress: What if the next generation of models shows diminishing returns? What if the boards of companies actually start asking questions about ROI instead of giving everyone a panicked AI Mandate?

Continued spend is independent of the model improvement rate. A true “off switch” to the AI buildout would require the revenue to stop flowing, and for that to happen, models would need to stop improving entirely. GPT-6 could be only, say, twice as capable as GPT-5 instead of an order of magnitude on some arbitrary set of benchmarks, a diminishing return is still a positive one. Even if we were in a slowdown scenario, inference demand will still be growing rapidly,[a] which would encourage the buildout of more datacenters.

Some would claim that inference demand growing isn't sufficient to guarantee the AI datacenter buildout. They claim that "on-device" inference will absorb this demand and therefore chill the CapEx buildout. This is misguided: on-device inference is a very different market than datacenter inference. On-device inference will be used to summarize your iMessage history. Datacenter inference will be used to automate white collar work. Those naturally require different workloads.

Second, on infrastructure bottlenecks: Another counterargument is that the development cycle for GPUs is fast, but the corresponding "deployment cycle" for said GPUs is slow. If GPUs are sitting idle between utility failures, equipment delivery issues, and labor shortages, then their usage will be limited to inference, not training. Projections for underwriting GPUs require both inference and training runs, and larger training runs power better models, which in turn propel the spend/hype/valuation lift/more spend cycle.

This is a clever point, but the hyperscalers can brute force through a lot of it. Google just bought a Texan datacenter energy company, Intersect, for $4.75 billion. Additionally, Stargate, for which the first site has already been "up and running" since September of 2025, will continue to come online, providing more training capacity for one of the leading frontier LLM labs. A non-trivial portion of Stargate's capacity is also in the Gulf, which not only does not suffer from the energy or permitting problems that many Western countries face for setting up training datacenters, but also has an infinite money printer of its own: natural gas and oil reserves, which it plans to spend on AI infrastructure.

Third, on geopolitical risk: The most serious objection. What about TSMC? What about the concentration risk? "Have you thought of that?"

Yes, I've thought of that. Everyone in this industry is well aware that China is a geopolitical threat to the sole maker of advanced chips and a huge chunk of the world's AI server manufacturing capacity. But this is not new information, and the broader industry is already responding to these fears. TSMC is building in Arizona and Japan, Samsung is expanding in Texas, and all of the hyperscalers are diversifying.

A Taiwan crisis severe enough to truly halt the AI buildout would also be severe enough to halt everything else. Maybe someone smarter than me is able to plan their way through a great power war, but for now I think a few smart hedges (which everyone is already doing!) will be enough.

But here's the thing: you can't capture a market with a robot that doesn't exist yet. So, we'll need to talk about the minimum viable robot, or MVR for short.

The Minimum Viable Robot (MVR)

The most underrated part of modern robotics is not the emergence of a general purpose humanoid, but rather the realization that cheap custom automation is now easier than ever.

The MVR is a reflection of this new reality. The MVR has parallel jaw grippers instead of dexterous hands for a lower teleoperation embodiment gap and wheels or no base at all instead of legs. The MVR uses a frontier open-source VLA, like pi-0.5, post-trained on custom datasets. VLAs don't even require that much capital to reach strong performance, since they were developed amid an ongoing lack of real-world robotic data, maintaining small pre-trained action heads.

The MVR can also be teleoperated, enabling deployment into the real world as soon as possible, bridging to full autonomy. The MVR is not perfectly general-purpose and may look unattractive in many cases, but it does have one thing going for it: it has a payback time measured in months instead of years.

Once your robot is working for enough hours, the economics flip from being dominated by CapEx to being dominated by OpEx. And OpEx is dominated by two things: speed and teleoperation.

If your robot is doing repeatable tasks in a scoped environment, you can get rid of the teleoperation requirement with enough data. The speed problem can be adjusted for with techniques such as SpeedTuning, SpeedAug, advantage-conditioned RL, and just raw data. Both the teleoperation problem and the speed problem will improve from both deployments and the underlying technology advancing. Additionally, the MVR can work 24 hours a day at near-minimal marginal cost. I'm purposely skipping over maintenance costs here, since we'll cover that in the section “Collective Intelligence,” but that isn't a game change either.

If your payback time is six months, you can outcompete human labor almost anywhere in the world within a year. If it's twelve months, you're still profitable by year two. Therefore, the way to win the MVR game is to get your payback time as low as possible, and then deploy said MVRs as aggressively as possible.

The Scale Of The Opportunity

And speaking of aggression, I know that I mentioned the numbers surrounding the AI datacenter buildout were staggering before, but let’s just start throwing out some big ones. Anthropic now has $50B in datacenter commitments with FluidStack. Google has a new $40B Texas buildout. xAI is planning to spend another $18B to expand their new datacenter, Colossus 2. Meta, Microsoft, and Amazon have all agreed to commit tens of billions of dollars a year for the next few years. Only 13GW out of the existing (not even accounting for new demand) 125GW of datacenter capacity has been converted to GPU computing. The amount of rising consumer, enterprise, sovereign, video model, and training needs sets this on fire, and, best of all, in the US every single part of this buildout can be fully depreciated in a year. Yes, you're reading that correctly. Under the OBBA's 100% bonus depreciation clause, qualifying equipment (for which both robots and AI infrastructure count) can be fully depreciated in one year instead of 5-7 years for tax benefits. There is no limit to this clause, and, anecdotally, accountants around the country have not wasted a second using this to write off everything under the sun that could count.

The kicker? All of these projects are desperate for labor.

Oracle has publicly talked abou[b]t pushing timelines back because they can't find enough workers. GPUs are sitting idle between utility failures, equipment delivery issues, and labor shortages.

That’s where the MVR comes in.

The AI Cartel

Here's the thing that makes this opportunity unusual: the companies building datacenters are the same companies building the AI that powers robotics. NVIDIA, the hyperscalers, the frontier labs: they all have skin in the game on both sides. A robotics company selling to this market can find not only a customer but also a partner with similarly aligned incentives. These buyers understand the technology roadmap because they're building it: they know that a robot that's not fully autonomous today will be autonomous in the future because they're the ones helping to train the models that will get it there. They're willing to deploy early, tolerate imperfection, and iterate because every deployment generates the data that makes the next deployment better, and that virtuous cycle benefits their core AI business too.

This is the AI cartel at work: a natural alignment of incentives that makes datacenters not just a good market, but perhaps the best market where early-stage robotics companies can find sophisticated buyers who understand the bet they're making.

The Four Market Segments [c]

Datacenter maintenance is smaller than the other submarkets I'm about to cover, but it's the easiest entry point for the MVR to succeed at. The work here is repetitive, scoped, and high-value. Automated inspection, such as cameras on mobile bases, are already proven tech, while tasks such as cable management, rack maintenance, datacenter logistics, and moving hardware between zones do not require a general-purpose humanoid. You just need something that works 24/7 in harsh conditions. Additionally, while the reliability requirements aren't necessarily trivial, they are doable in production systems today (there are many stealth robotic startups already deployed in this market). Also, unlike self-driving cars, there's no catastrophic failures to be had here, so there's no reason for this to be held up.

The next opportunity up the difficulty curve is supply chain and manufacturing: specifically, the assembly of all the equipment that goes into datacenters. Even small datacenters can have hundreds of thousands of electrical components which make up IT racks, servers, networking devices, power equipment units, cooling units, and more. Foxconn now makes more money on AI servers than iPhones. NVIDIA's networking business is now its fastest growing segment, and is also [d]shifting over to an 800 VDC power architecture starting in 2027, which means that over 13GW (and growing) of already installed GPU clusters will need to be upgraded.

Why is this easier than construction? Because it happens in controlled factory environments where you own the conditions. The robots aren't dealing with weather, uneven terrain, or constantly changing jobsites. It's repeatable work in a fixed location where maintenance costs can be amortized efficiently. The entire global electronics supply chain is reorienting itself around AI infrastructure. And the same robots that can assemble servers can assemble electronics for phones, cars, appliances, you name it! It's all the same companies, the ODMs, doing it anyway. You just have to walk down the hall to set up a new line.

Datacenter construction is the bigger market, but it's also the most complex. It helps to think about it in two phases: post-shell and pre-shell.

Post-shell work is what happens after the building envelope is complete. This includes setting up the networking infrastructure, installing HVAC systems, running cables, and mounting equipment. Post-shell work is significantly more tractable for robotics because the environment is controlled, the tasks are more standardized, and there's less coordination with external factors. Depending on your design, 40-85% of all compon[e]ents in an AI datacenter can be prefabricated and installed in post-shell: racks, cooling units, power distribution, networking gear. This matches perfectly with the reality of where robotics technology is today. A robot installing prefabricated server racks in a climate-controlled shell is doing work that's fundamentally similar to what robots already do in warehouses and factories.

Pre-shell work is harder. This is the structural, electrical, and plumbing work that happens before you have a finished building, such as pouring foundations, erecting steel, and running conduit through concrete. Pre-shell construction involves bespoke designs, constantly changing conditions, heavy equipment coordination, and a thicket of permitting and inspection requirements. Each site has different soil conditions, different utility connections, and different local codes. Unlike Katerra, the infamous prefabrication construction startup that failed due to a lack of product-market fit, the post-shell opportunity actually has immense potential for product-market fit because you're standardizing where standardization makes sense while leaving the genuinely bespoke work to humans. For now.

The path forward is clear: start with post-shell, where robots can work on prefabricated and modular components in controlled conditions, then gradually expand into pre-shell as the technology matures. I expect that the HVAC and electrician industries, which are intertwined with the construction industry, will follow a similar path, starting with highly scoped or prefabricated work before moving to enterprise deployment sites and finally individual jobs.

The Flywheel

All of this means that the AI datacenter buildout is going to give robotics companies something they've never had before: a market that is large, fast-growing, high-margin, and willing to pay premium prices for solutions that work run by sophisticated buyers who actually understand technology.

We've all seen what the infinite money printer did for LLM research. I don't think enough people have fully internalized what it's about to do for robotics.

In the AI datacenter buildout we have discovered our infinite money printer that we will point at our data problem, our supply chain problem, our R&D problem, and whatever new problem we'll discover along the way.

The robots will build the AI infrastructure which will train the models to advance the robots which will continue to build the AI infrastructure; a self-reinforcing loop where the future retrocausally reaches[f] back to assemble itself.

Nothing will stop the coming buildout.

Nothing will stand in the way of robotics eating the world.

Nothing…except, perhaps, that data problem we mentioned earlier.

Deploy, Deploy, Deploy

Deploying robots isn't a footnote to robotics research, it's the central insight that should guide every decision we make.

The marginal cost of real-world data drops dramatically once you have robots in the field. This is why the AI datacenter buildout markets matter so much. The sooner we can get thousands of robots out into the real world, the faster we can generate massive datasets that start a flywheel: model improvement, increased company and industry-wide morale, customer relationships, lower cost of capital, and many other wonderful things.

But getting to deployment requires solving the central question of robotics research: how much real-world human data do we actually need?

The Data Problem

Real-world data, whether collected through leader-follower teleoperation or UMI gloves, is expensive and difficult to collect at the quantity, quality, and diversity a "robotic AGI" would require.

Here's where things get interesting. The scaling laws for robotics data are non-trivial. Unlike language models, which tend to do better as their number of model parameters, dataset size, and compute get scaled up, what makes a capable robot is much harder to capture. Moravec's paradox captures well: it's comparatively easy to make computers exhibit adult-level performance on intelligence tests, but difficult or impossible to give them the skills of a one-year-old when it comes to perception and mobility. Data diversity and quality matter more than volume after a threshold. Recovery sequences are particularly valuable: a demonstration where the robot drops something and picks it up teaches more than ten perfect executions. Multiple camera angles have also been shown to help.

So, what kind of data can, and should, we use to train our robots?

If we could leverage other sources, egocentric videos, third-person data from TikTok/YouTube, or simulations, our job would be easier. Instead of relying solely on the painfully complex, capital-intensive work of manual data collectors, we could leverage massive GPU clusters to improve our models. Cloud computing is one of the very few markets where tens of billions of dollars can be spent productively, which has played no small part in the success of the LLM research community.

We’ll cover how both sources of data will be important, but we’re first going to deal with the real world.

The Collection Revolution

My favorite example of progress is how painful collecting robotic data used to be. You once needed expensive custom hardware and highly trained operators, but even with that, your work wasn't guaranteed to be replicable by other researchers.

Now we have ALOHA, which made a reliable teleoperation setup cheap; UMI, which showed you could collect manipulation data with a handheld gripper that cost next to nothing; and Diffusion Policy, which made imitation learning dramatically easier to train.

Diffusion Policy deserves special attention because making imitation learning easier to train is dramatically powerful. The "search space" of robotics, from a joint text-image input to all possible actions, is incredibly vast, but imitation learning on human data compresses this search space to something

reasonable.

It's a huge key to why modern robotics works, and so it's no wonder that when Tony Zhao (the inventor of ALOHA) and Cheng Chi (the inventor of UMI and Diffusion Policy) started Sunday Robotics together, they created another breakthrough. Sunday's "Skill Capture Glove," a variation of an UMI-style gripper that looks like a wearable claw, mimics Sunday's end-effector so the model can learn fluid, natural motions directly from human demonstrations without a significant embodiment gap.

Of course, the problem of state gaps, the difference between a human demonstration and what a robot policy learns, still exists. This is a problem for UMI-style grippers, which need high software QA and calibration to work after shipping, and for teleoperation setups, which can have failures like humans using the top camera in teleoperation while robots heavily rely on wrist cameras. Yet I believe even these problems will be solved in a few years.

First, the design space for teleoperation and UMI grippers is vast and easy to iterate on. An entire ecosystem of companies has popped up trying to supply real-world data to robotics companies. Many of these companies, such as MicroAGI and Build AI, are turning their eyes to egocentric data.

Second, as an interesting side effect of data collection getting easier, there's now a fast-growing open-source community in robotics. This community not only collects open datasets for HuggingFace but constantly iterates on hardware setups. The BEHAVIOR challenge at NeurIPS exemplifies this "hacker spirit" that has been present in LLM research (NanoGPT, llama.cpp) but not in robotics. The open-source community in LLM research has been very useful for new architectural ideas (multi-head latent attention came from DeepSeek, a famously open-source company). NVIDIA, Physical Intelligence, and more recently Amazon FAR have been publishing great open-source research and models. In the long run, I expect this community to resemble the AI community: open-source robotics will be prolific and produce many great businesses but will not be at the frontier or capture the vast majority of value.

Why? Because since data collection has shifted from "how do we collect any data at all" to "how do we collect enough data fast enough," robotics has become a problem that scales with money and people. And as great as real-world data is, it still doesn't scale as fast as many would like.

Enter: Egocentric Data

The most primitive form of egocentric data is an iPhone strapped to someone's head collecting data while they do tasks. You can increase this data's quality dramatically with both hardware and software techniques, and best of all, it's incredibly cheap, scalable, and unobtrusive compared to teleoperation or even UMI-style gloves. This matters enormously for robotic generalization.

So, can we just melt all the GPUs now [g]and forget about the real world? Not exactly.

Sergey Levine is correct: "What consistently works best in machine learning is to ensure that the training data matches the test conditions,” which is the real world. But egocentric and video data working means our real-world deployments now generate a lot more value.

Additionally, we can get increasing value by replacing the VLM backbone of VLAs with video backbones. Early work has shown dramatic increases in sample efficiency (as much as 10x), although that work is still early. And if video backbones are becoming workable, that opens up exciting possibilities. If you were at NeurIPS this year, you would've heard through the whisper networks that viewpoint transformation, the conversion of regular videos, first or third person, into egocentric views, is now becoming tractable as well.

We are about to have an explosion of new data, which will favor large AI incumbents (who can transfer their GPU clusters and expertise training large multimodal models) but also startups clever about getting the "right kind of real-world data" that can be amplified.

How many robots would it take to cross the total amount of videos YouTube has? It's a fascinating question that highlights both the scale of the challenge and the opportunity. YouTube has over 800 million videos. But robots generating continuous, structured, task-oriented data in real environments produce something qualitatively different, and potentially more valuable per hour, than the average cat video.

Simulation and World Models

We haven't covered simulations, but the same logic applies there as well. Sim-to-real transfer isn't as prohibitive as it used to be. Techniques such as domain randomization (which was typically used for locomotion research but shows promise now), physics-accurate simulation, and generative models for scene diversity all reduce the real-world data needed for certain capabilities. A fun technique I haven't seen integrated enough into robotics simulations is dynamic foveated rendering, which saves compute by not wasting visual resources where the robot can't see, redirecting those resources to physical simulation instead.

But are physical simulations even the right approach? Perhaps not. The traditional approach to simulation, whether using MuJoCo, Isaac Sim, Genesis, PyBullet, involves a hand-coded physics engine that will always have a reality gap. Learned world models could flip this, because instead of coding the physics, we could learn a model of how the world works from collected data.

Papers like GSWorld are using Gaussian splats to reconstruct real environments and then simulate robot interactions within them.

All you need is a few minutes of deployment environment video to generate synthetic training data that looks like your actual environment, not a generic simulated warehouse, which shrinks the reality gap. This is still early, and world models have issues with controllability, robustness, multimodality, long-horizon reasoning, and overfitting, just like other ML subareas. But the trajectory is clear: as these models improve, they'll become yet another multiplier on your real-world data.

Maybe this is how error correction ultimately happens, like how CoT error correction emerged in language models. Imitation learning teaches some instances of recovering, and then an RL stage optimizing for set outcomes reinforces those circuits.

Optimizing for Deployment

All of this points back to deployment. Optimizing for deployment reveals an underexplored research area: chucking everything we can into our pretrained models. The action heads for most VLAs are tiny, usually less than a billion parameters, so they can be pre-trained and post-trained quickly.

I expect pretraining papers like Posterior Behavior Cloning (PBC), which helps with credit assignment in imitation learning, or Spatial Forcing, which aligns VLA visual tokens with 3D spatial representations using a 30-loc cosine similarity loss, will become more widespread. I also expect post-training papers, like Physical Intelligence's pi*0.6, to explode now that we finally have the data to shove everything into a model. That will also, unintentionally, start making progress on long-horizon reasoning, which was near-impossible for robotics due to lack of hardware reliability and adequate data.

The Generalization Problem

A valid criticism of modern robotics research is that it's faking a lot of generalization. Physical Intelligence's recent entrance to the "robotic olympics" had to be fine-tuned on every individual task rather than doing it straight from the pretrained model. Many papers claim to possess a general-purpose model that fails the moment there's a scene change. Yes, humans are jagged and not perfectly general, but our robots are still much worse than humans at generalization, something necessary for them to become truly transformative.

This is a real challenge, but progress is being made and we know where to push.

Results from reputable companies like Sunday's "pick up anything" demo are incredibly promising forms of generalization: it seems you can get affordances for free just by training on human data. It's also more practically useful than a number going up on some complex benchmark acronym. Simon Willison agrees, arguing that model companies like Anthropic shouldn't just release improvements on SWE-Bench but also a list of things that Claude Code couldn't do before but can do now.

A point in favor of generalization comes from the self-driving car industry. This industry is not perfectly general, but it's having a real revenue inflection because it has brute-forced a lot of the data collection problem while ramping up simulation efforts. Robotics will also face a "problem of the nines" to eat the world, but we'll have an infinite money printer, scoped environments, and a relative lack of downside risk on our side.

Papers such as ReWiND, Latent Policy Barrier (LPB), SAILOR, and Google DeepMind's "Self-improving embodied foundation models" further point to brute force being a viable way to get more generalization, as they've all increased model robustness and sample efficiency.

And as the old saying goes, there are no new problems in machine learning, only new datasets.

Robotics will not hit a wall. But walls aren't the only obstacle to look out for. Even if you have the market and the data, you still need to actually make the robots. And that means we need to talk about supply chains.

Collective Intelligence

Supply chain analysis is an inherently Straussian endeavor. An analyst’s job is to read through thousands of pages of documents, often dense government documents, in search of the single insight which will grant them an edge.

For robotics, that insight is this: the supply chain is already sufficient for the coming buildout. The real threats are found in the tail risks: geopolitics, irrational rare earths processing monopolies, and precision reducer bottlenecks. While the collective intelligence of capital markets, governments, and manufacturing ecosystems are already mobilizing against these risks, the question is: can they move fast enough to avoid the tail risks?

Rare Earths

If you go across the stack of robotics (energy, commodities, minerals, precision mechanical components, batteries, motors, wires, end-effectors, electronics, cameras, on-board compute, subassemblies, full assemblies) you’ll notice that minerals are upstream of almost everything. Actuators need motors, motors need magnets, and magnets need rare earths.  

China controls roughly 85% of global rare earth processing and 90% of rare earth magnet production, having built this dominance decades ago by subsidizing their domestic industry and driving everyone else out of business. There is a serious concentration risk here.


But the risk has materialized, and the response is already underway. China has already imposed export controls on rare earths. Just this year China has taken action in April, which was luckily followed by a tariff truce, October, with the foreign direct product rule (FDPR) requiring licenses even for foreign-made products incorporation Chinese-origin rare earths, and December, largely denying export licenses to companies with any affiliation to foreign militaries. Additionally, while they’re not rare earths, silver prices have been skyrocketing recently[h] on the news that China will restrict silver exports only to companies with a government license in 2026.

Therefore, it’s not surprising that many countries want to diversify off of the Chinese supply of rare earths, especially considering that the phrase "rare earths" is a misnomer: they aren't actually rare. Significant deposits exist in the United States, Australia, Brazil, India, Vietnam, and elsewhere. Geological scarcity isn’t the issue, China’s concentrated processing infrastructure that stands alone after decades of subsidies made competition uneconomical is the issue.

The processing itself isn't magic. But that's not the same as saying it's easy. ASML's machines are also "just optics,” in that the underlying physics is well-understood, but their engineering moat is enormous. The right framework for understanding rare earths processing is that the chemistry is liquid-liquid separation and solvent extraction, which are both things that the oil and gas industry does at massive scale every day. The specific separation chemistries for rare earths are difficult, but the operational expertise, such as running large-scale continuous chemical processes, handling hazardous materials, managing regulatory complexity, can directly transfer here.

So, why didn't oil and gas companies do this decades ago? Because there was no reason to: Chinese subsidies made rare earth processing uneconomical for everyone else, and no strategic imperative existed to change that. Standard Oil also famously built its monopoly on refining, not on oil itself, and held enormous power until a combination of US antitrust and European diversification broke its hold. While China will not break up its processing capabilities, the incentives to diversify have never been stronger.

Trump, Vance, and Lutnick have spoken about a shared vision for reshored American manufacturing, powered by robotics. JPMorgan Chase has a $1.5 trillion Security and Resilience program with rare earths as a focus. MP Materials, an American company, saw the Department of Defense take a $400 million equity stake, making the U.S. government its largest shareholder, plus a 10-year price floor commitment and a $150 million loan to expand heavy rare earth separation capacity. Lynas, the world’s largest non-Chinese producer of rare earths, recently achieved the first commercial separation of heavy rare earths (dysprosium and terbium) outside China in decades. The infinite money printer is pointed at this problem. Processing capacity will be built in years, not decades.

Of course, the sophisticated bear case isn’t that rare earths are unsolvable, it’s just that they won’t be ramped up in time. Building physical infrastructure is hard,[i] so that’s a fair take. But the inventory buffer is real, the capital is being deployed, and new plants are under construction. The risk here is a temporary squeeze, not a structural dependence. Money and willpower can solve the rare earths problem: it just takes time. Precision reducers are a different beast entirely, though.

Precision Reducers

A precision reducer (also called a gearbox or gear reducer) is a mechanical device that reduces the speed of a motor while increasing its torque. In robotics, reducers sit between motors and joints, converting high-speed, low-torque motor output into the slow, powerful, precise movements that robot limbs require. The two main types are harmonic drives (using a flexible gear that deforms inside a rigid outer gear) and RV reducers (using a planetary gear system). Both require extraordinary manufacturing precision, tolerances measured in microns, to minimize backlash (unwanted play in the gears) and ensure smooth, accurate motion over thousands of hours of operation.

The Japanese conglomerates Harmonic Drive and Nabtesco dominate the market here: Nabtesco alone holds 60% of the global market for RV reducers, with even higher shares in medium and heavy-duty applications. These components are a pain to replace, or scale, because certain failure modes only emerge over thousands of hours of operation, over multi-year qualification lead times. Tribology, the study of friction and interacting surfaces, is a devilishly complex multi-scale problem that spans the molecular to the macroscopic, and is highly empirical and underdeveloped. You cannot buy your way out of this one.

Or can you?

Why Reducers Are Solvable

The implicit assumption here is that we need Harmonic Drive-quality reducers for every application in robotics. But we don’t for every application. Most of the tasks that robots are doing are not brain surgery: we can use cheaper, lower-precision components and just replace them more frequently. The higher-order bit for what makes a useful robot is the capability of the underlying AI model, and if that’s generating enough revenue, then swapping out a relatively cheap reducer once a year isn’t that bad.

Second, really good software can compensate for hardware imprecisions. This is the whole point of learning-based approaches: you can adapt to whatever embodiment you’re in. The ALOHA leader-follower system runs on cheap motors with 3D-printed gears. Physical Intelligence’s recent work shows that you can do real manipulation tasks with simple hardware. The skeptic might counter by arguing that research demos and industrial reliability expectations are very different ballparks, but I believe this should still hold up. Most industrial failures are due to integration and software issues, not precision reducer wear and tear. Even FANUC, one of the GOATs of industrial robotics, has its components replaced on a regular schedule. A slightly worse reducer will not kill you.

Third, Chinese manufacturers are catching up fast. Green Harmonic, Leaderdrive, Zhongda Leader, and Shuanghuan Transmission are all shipping reducers at ~80-90% of Japanese quality for ~30-40% of the price. This matters not because American companies will necessarily use Chinese reducers directly. For a variety of geopolitical and supply chain security reasons, many won't. Instead, this matters because this is an example of the “four-minute mile” effect: Once the barrier is broken, others follow. China has proven cheap, similar-enough quality reducers can be manufactured, which means that factories in Vietnam and Malaysia can fast follow. The ecosystems, quality control cultures, and supplier relationships in Bac Ninh and Penang are deeply underrated. Lower-quality harmonic reducers aren't rocket science; the knowledge diffuses through reverse engineering and upfront capital commitments.

Fourth, reducer qualification cycles can be compressed by running in parallel with deployment. Every hour of field operation is an hour of qualification data. The multi-year timelines I mentioned above assume that you qualify your parts before you deploy. If you qualify your parts during deployment, accepting higher early replacement rates in exchange for faster iteration, then the math here changes entirely. If this approach is good enough for SpaceX and Tesla, then it’s also good enough for us.

Chinese Competition

But any serious supply chain analysis must also acknowledge that China isn’t just a components supplier but also an end product manufacturer as well. Unitree has achieved what many consider to be the best locomotion capabilities in the industry, with full-body teleoperation requiring minimal external equipment. Companies like UBTECH, Fourier Intelligence, and dozens of others are racing to production as we speak, and Chinese humanoids are already shipping at price points ($10-20K)[j] that Western manufacturers currently can’t match.

This creates two strategic dilemmas. The first is that capital cannot buy clock speed: dumping even 100 billion dollars at a time into domestic or allied manufacturing efforts will not work unless the surrounding ecosystem is similarly strong. The second is that China’s fast-moving, upstream ecosystem has also created the traceability trap. Companies paranoid about having Chinese components, whether because of backdoors, data leakage, or supply chain risk, in their products often can’t verify[k] where parts actually originate. Therefore, deals are frequently being done today with unresolved questions about what happens when a key vendor disappears or gets sanctioned.

The EV supply chain

This is why I believe that much of the supply chain for robotics will converge with the electric vehicle supply chain. The enterprise traceability infrastructure, expertise, and scale necessary for the robotics buildout already exists here. Tesla understands this, which is why their Optimus robot is built on an automotive manufacturing base, with mostly the same factories, suppliers, and processes being used. Tesla has also talked about the Von Neumann self-replicating machine concept: robots making robots throughout the stack, not just at final assembly.  It’s not as crazy as it sounds: FANUC has already been doing this since 2001 in its “lights-out” factories, where FANUC robot arms are manufacturing FANUC robot arms. Granted, the conditions are very tightly controlled, but robots which build robots aren't sci-fi.

This convergence means that you need to be in Asia if you want to be a serious player in robotics. If your hardware strategy consists of ordering Uber Eats and 3D-printing parts in your nondescript San Francisco or Los Angeles warehouse, then you are going to be crushed by the people who are booking it to Bac Ninh right now.  

Geographic Strategies

Of course, just saying “you need to be in Asia,” is incredibly vague. Where should you go? Well, Vietnam is the obvious China-plus-one, with its low cost of labor, educated workforce, and eager government, so relationships there are getting competitive. Everyone from Samsung to SpaceX’s Starlink components supply chain is moving to Vietnam.

So, given this, I actually think Taiwan is quite underrated: everyone avoids it for their supply chains because of an invasion risk, but, again, if Taiwan gets invaded, the entire world is going to go nuts, and you can obviously diversify as a hedge. But even better, as we mentioned in The Infinite Money Printer section, there is a massive market to be found in servicing the supply chain for the AI datacenter buildout. If your custo[l]mer and your manufacturer are the same person, then that certainly makes your feedback loops faster, particularly as cheaper hardware and better AI obviate the traditional barriers to automation, such as skepticism, opportunity costs, software complexity, tooling design, custom fixtures, long bring-up times, and electrical integration.

Malaysia offers similar advantages, with Penang's semiconductor and electronics manufacturing ecosystem providing deep bench strength in precision assembly. Japan and South Korea similarly  remain options but aren't as central to the AI infrastructure boom as Taiwan.

Conclusion

But putting it all together: the supply chain problems in robotics are real but solvable. Rare earths are a capital and expertise problem: the capital is deployed, the expertise exists in oil and gas, and China’s aggressive use of export controls has only accelerated the diversification it hoped to prevent. Precision reducers are a qualification and precision problem that software compensation, good-enough alternatives, and deployment-stage qualification erode away. The broader supply chain is converging with the automotive and electronics supply chain, which means the infrastructure is already there for companies willing to plant roots in Asia.


Additionally, Chinese robotics companies like Unitree represent serious competition not just as suppliers but as end-product manufacturers with cost and speed advantages that Western companies struggle to match. This reality demands acknowledgment. Yet those who think that supply chains will block the robotics buildout, or enable China to dominate, are making a timing argument, not a structural one. And the timing is shorter than they think: the infinite money printer is turning on, the diversification is happening, and the technology is growing more forgiving of hardware imperfections.

The collective intelligence of capital markets, governments, and manufacturing ecosystems is working at full force to solve these problems.

The supply chain will hold, which means we should talk about what happens when all of this actually works.

The Final Offshoring

We are about to enter a world where the marginal cost of labor approaches zero.

That sentence sounds simple, but labor having a marginal cost is so deeply embedded in how we organize our society, cities, homes, relationships, and identities that most people can't quite see what changes when it disappears.

The answer is: almost everything.

But the most interesting implication of the final offshoring is that human preferences, rather than labor scarcity, will be the dominant factor for how society is structured.

That bottleneck starts at the smallest scale, your home, and will ripple outward to reshape economies, cultures, and nations.

We’ll first cover why this wave of automation is different from ones before it, then get into some of the trades (investments) one could do relating to this wave.

Then I’ll start analyzing the impacts of the final offshoring, starting at the home level before successively covering culture, markets, and geopolitics.

Why This Time Is Different

There are three reasons which stand out.  

The first is that previous automation was very narrow (ATM, Tractors, FANUC robot arms) while robotics will be very general.

This will make robotics easy to integrate and able to do jobs that would’ve otherwise been too expensive or impossible for custom automation to do.

The second is that two robots deployed in different industries can learn from each other (“transfer learning”), while prior forms of automation could not.

The third is that, as seen from the previous portions of this essay, the problems of “markets, data, and supply chains” will not be major blockers here.

Therefore, dismissing robotics as "Silicon Valley hype" will be a costly mistake to make. Do not make it.

Utopia’s Paradox[r][s][t]

Optimistic predictions about the future will always get scrutinized far more harshly than pessimistic ones, as being wrong about good things makes you look foolish, while being wrong about bad things makes you look prudent. This reputational asymmetry thus naturally biases “serious” people toward being pessimists.

Modern neuroscience research agrees; Karl Friston proposes a general explanation for this negativity bias whereby the brain attempts to minimize dangerous surprises through balancing prediction accuracy to information seeking, often favoring the former over the latter.

This isn’t necessarily a bad instinct to have: being considerate of corner cases, hedges, and caution in the face of blind hype is a valuable skill to have. But it’s not the only skill that matters. If we really are about to enter a world where the marginal cost of labor goes to zero, then a lot of our assumptions about what is practical and sensible begin to go out the window.

This skepticism creates “utopia’s paradox[u]:” even though many desire a good future, conceptualizing and then working towards this good future is nearly impossible to achieve.

This is not just because the word Utopia itself, deriving from the Greek words “ou-topos” (no place) and “eu-topos” (good place), is inherently ironic.
